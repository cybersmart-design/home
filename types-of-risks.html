Types of Risks & Threats

Cybersecurity doesn't have to be confusing. Let's break down the most common digital risks and learn how to protect yourself online.

Start Learning

Malware (The Digital Sickness)

Malware, short for "malicious software," is like a digital cold for your devices. It includes viruses and ransomware designed to sneak onto your computer, break your systems, or steal your private information.

Source: Simplified from the Cybersecurity & Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA)

How to stay safe: Keep your apps and devices updated, use reliable antivirus software, and always back up your important files.

Phishing (The Digital Disguise)

Phishing is a scam where criminals send fake emails or texts pretending to be a person or company you trust. Their goal is to trick you into clicking a dangerous link or typing in your passwords.

Source: Simplified from the Cybersecurity & Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA)

How to stay safe: Double-check email addresses, never click suspicious links, and turn on two-factor authentication (2FA) for your accounts.

Data Breaches (The Digital Break-In)

A data breach happens when hackers successfully break into a secure database and steal private information. This could include passwords, credit card numbers, or personal messages.

Source: Simplified from IBM

How to stay safe: Use long, unique passwords for every account so that if one website gets breached, your other accounts stay locked.

Social Engineering (The Mind Game)

Instead of hacking computers, social engineering "hacks" people. Attackers use lies and manipulation to build trust, convincing you to break safety rules or hand over sensitive information voluntarily.

Source: Simplified from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)

How to stay safe: Always be skeptical of urgent requests for money or personal details, and verify the person's identity before helping.

Denial of Service - DoS (The Traffic Jam)

A Denial of Service (DoS) attack creates a massive digital traffic jam. Attackers flood a website or network with so much fake traffic that it overloads and crashes, blocking real users from getting in.

Source: Simplified from the Cybersecurity & Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA)

How to stay safe: While mostly a threat to website owners, everyday users can help by keeping devices virus-free so they aren't secretly used to generate this fake traffic.
